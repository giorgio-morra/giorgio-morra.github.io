Project summary

This project focuses on the structural vibration analysis and modal parameter extraction of mechanical systems. The work began by developing a foundational mathematical model to compute the natural frequencies, mode shapes, and Frequency Response Functions (FRFs) of a continuous cantilever beam.

Building upon this theoretical framework, multi-mode curve fitting algorithms were applied to a real-world experimental case study: a light rail wheel. The engineering objective was to investigate the axial vibrations of the wheel surface, which act similarly to a loudspeaker and cause significant acoustic radiation and noise pollution.

The wheel was tested in a free-free configuration suspended by elastic supports. Experimental data was processed from a dynamometric impact hammer and 12 piezoelectric accelerometers spaced at 15-degree intervals. By utilizing non-linear least squares optimization routines, the resonance peaks were successfully isolated from the quasi-static and seismographic residuals, allowing accurate numerical reconstruction and visualization of physical mode shapes directly from the raw accelerometer data.

Key contributions

  • Developed a modal model for natural frequency, mode shape, and FRF prediction of a continuous beam.
  • Applied multi-mode curve fitting to experimental light rail wheel data.
  • Designed a free-free test configuration with elastic suspension and 12 accelerometer channels.
  • Used nonlinear least squares to isolate resonances and reconstruct physical mode shapes from raw data.
