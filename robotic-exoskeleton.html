Project summary

This project focuses on the development and implementation of a one-degree-of-freedom (1-DoF) collaborative robotic exoskeleton. The device is specifically engineered to assist with elbow flexion-extension rehabilitation for patients recovering from acute post-stroke conditions.

It is intended for individuals with severe motor disabilities, specifically those who have a Muscle Research Council (MRC) score of 2 or lower for their biceps and triceps, meaning they cannot perform active movements against gravity. To overcome the resource-intensive and inconsistent nature of traditional manual therapy, the exoskeleton employs a user-friendly "teach-and-play" control strategy that automates repetitive therapeutic motions.

Key points

  • Teach-and-play strategy: the rehabilitation approach is divided into two distinct phases to simplify customization without requiring manual coding.
  • Teach phase: a therapist manually guides the patient's arm through a therapeutic motion while the exoskeleton uses an "active-assistive" transparency mode to record the exact trajectory.
  • Play phase: the device switches to a "passive" rigid control mode where it autonomously and smoothly replicates the recorded trajectory for the patient.
  • Hardware setup: the system is operated via an Arduino Nano 33 IoT micro-controller, actuated by an electric servo motor equipped with an angle sensor, and uses a force sensor on the wrist to read the therapist's inputs.
